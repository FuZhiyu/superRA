## Objective

You get derivations a referee can audit line by line: every symbol defined, every assumption interpretable, every step justified. The discipline is **four gates, walked in the order your trust depends on — Objects & Notation → Assumptions → Derivations → Verification & Rendering.**

The order is the point. You can't judge an assumption that rests on an undefined symbol, and you can't trust a derivation without knowing which assumptions are active in it — so the agent defines its objects, fixes its assumptions, and only then manipulates equations, with verification and rendering last. Each gate is a checkpoint the work passes before the next one opens, which is also how the reviewer reads it: notation before assumptions, assumptions before algebra. This guards against the characteristic failure of machine-generated math — plausible algebra resting on a symbol that quietly means two things, or an assumption back-filled after the fact to make a step go through.

Reach for it when a task derives, solves, verifies, or proves something mathematical: stating assumptions, writing first-order conditions, solving an equilibrium, running comparative statics, checking a proof, or producing renderable model notes.

For the full gate-by-gate discipline and the rendering conventions, see [theory-modeling](skills/theory-modeling/SKILL.md).
