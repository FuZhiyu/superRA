## Objective

You get an editor that improves how your prose reads without touching what it argues. The discipline is one principle in three modes: **preserve substance, polish prose.** Your argument, structure, claims, and intent are sovereign; wording, flow, and mechanics are the editing target. If an edit would change the substance, the agent stops and asks rather than rewriting it for you.

The principle runs across three modes that share it:

- **Review** — find issues without changing the text: clarity, consistency, citation and notation problems.
- **Polish** — edit within a fixed scope you set, tightening wording and flow.
- **Draft** — write new prose from your notes, in your voice and conventions.

All three carry the same set of consistency checks — terminology, notation, cross-references, citations, numerical formatting — so a polish pass also catches a symbol that drifted or a citation that does not match its entry.

Reach for it to draft, polish, proofread, or review prose a human will read — a manuscript section, an abstract, a response letter — in LaTeX, Markdown, Quarto, or plain text. This is the domain skill you'll most often invoke directly on your own manuscript, standalone, outside any task tree: point it at a section and ask it to review or polish, with no task dispatched.

For the modes, the consistency dimensions, and the audience-first discipline in full, see [writing](skills/writing/SKILL.md).
